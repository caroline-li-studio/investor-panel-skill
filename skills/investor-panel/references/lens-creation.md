# Lens Creation

Use this guide when adding or revising an investor lens. A lens is not a biography or imitation. It is a compact decision procedure that makes the agent ask different questions than the rest of the panel.

## Source Inputs

Use high-quality public material when possible:

- Shareholder letters, fund letters, books, interviews, lectures, memos, and case studies.
- Repeated investment examples, both wins and mistakes.
- Publicly stated risk controls, sell rules, and opportunity filters.
- Evidence about portfolio construction, concentration, time horizon, and preferred asset types.

Avoid turning a single famous quote or one investment example into the whole lens.

## Distillation Steps

1. **Circle of competence**: What kinds of companies, assets, markets, or situations does this lens understand best?
2. **Positive filter**: What evidence makes the lens interested?
3. **Negative filter**: What evidence makes the lens reject, downgrade, or say too-hard?
4. **Core question**: What question would this lens ask that other lenses might miss?
5. **Evidence standard**: What facts are required before the lens can speak with confidence?
6. **Time horizon**: Is the lens judging quarters, cycles, multi-year compounding, or permanent capital loss?
7. **Failure mode**: Where does this style tend to be wrong?

## Lens Format

Write each lens with:

- A one-line focus statement.
- Four to six operating rules.
- At least one explicit downgrade or veto condition.
- No first-person roleplay and no claim that the real investor would agree.

## Quality Bar

A good lens changes the analysis. It should produce different objections, required evidence, or confidence levels than neighboring lenses.

Reject or revise a lens if it only says "buy good companies cheaply", repeats another lens, relies on vague personality traits, or cannot name falsifiable kill criteria.
