# Investor Lenses

Use these lenses as analytical frameworks. Do not impersonate the investors or imply the output is their real opinion.

## Vote Format

Each lens should produce:

- `Signal`: bullish, neutral, bearish, watch, pass, hold, sell-risk, or too-hard.
- `Confidence`: 0-100, lowered when evidence is stale or incomplete.
- `Core reason`: one sentence.
- `Veto / kill criterion`: the fact that would make this lens reject or downgrade the idea.

## Aswath Damodaran Lens

Focus on narrative-to-numbers discipline.

- Translate the story into revenue growth, margin, reinvestment, cost of capital, terminal value, and dilution assumptions.
- Ask what the market must believe at the current price.
- Use ranges and scenarios; do not hide uncertainty behind a precise target price.
- Separate great company from great investment.
- Downgrade when plausible numbers cannot support the story or when the valuation requires near-perfect execution.

## Benjamin Graham Lens

Focus on conservative protection.

- Require a concrete margin of safety using earnings, assets, book value, net current assets, and balance sheet strength.
- Prefer low valuation, liquidity, modest leverage, stable earnings, and dividend history.
- Strong business quality does not excuse a speculative price.
- Downgrade when price is far above conservative intrinsic value, current ratio is weak, debt is high, or earnings are unstable.
- This lens is allowed to reject excellent compounders that are too expensive by defensive-investor standards.

## Bill Ackman Lens

Focus on concentrated, catalyst-driven quality and activism.

- Look for high-quality businesses where strategic, operational, capital allocation, or governance changes can unlock value.
- Prefer simple business models, strong brands, pricing power, recurring revenue, and clear catalysts.
- Ask what management or the board could do differently and whether shareholders can influence it.
- Downgrade when the catalyst is vague, governance is entrenched, leverage is excessive, or the thesis depends on public pressure rather than economics.

## Cathie Wood Lens

Focus on disruptive innovation and exponential growth.

- Ask whether the company is on the right side of a major technology cost curve, adoption curve, or platform shift.
- Prefer large expanding markets, rapid revenue growth, network effects, data advantages, and optionality.
- Accept near-term valuation discomfort only when the long-term market and margin structure can plausibly justify it.
- Downgrade when innovation is narrative-only, competition commoditizes the market, dilution is high, or growth assumptions are not anchored in evidence.

## Charlie Munger Lens

Focus on avoiding stupidity.

- Use inversion first: how does this investment lose money?
- Diagnose incentives, agency problems, accounting games, empire building, and crowd psychology.
- Prefer simple, high-quality, durable businesses that can be held for a long time.
- Use the too-hard pile when the business, technology, or policy risk cannot be understood well enough.
- Downgrade for leverage, promotional management, weak incentives, excessive complexity, or Lollapalooza-style hype.

## David Swensen Lens

Focus on portfolio construction, governance, and long-term stewardship.

- Ask what role the asset plays in a diversified portfolio: return driver, inflation hedge, liquidity reserve, diversifier, or opportunistic bet.
- Prefer durable institutions, aligned governance, disciplined capital allocation, and assets that can be held through cycles.
- Evaluate liquidity, rebalancing value, fee/friction drag, and whether the expected return compensates for illiquidity or complexity.
- Downgrade when the thesis depends on chasing recent returns, when governance is weak, or when liquidity risk is ignored.
- This lens may like an asset less as a standalone story if it adds poor portfolio-level diversification or unacceptable liquidity stress.

## David Tepper Lens

Focus on distressed value, capital structure survival, and policy turns.

- Look for panic pricing where the balance sheet can survive and policy, credit, or liquidity conditions are turning favorable.
- Map the capital structure, maturities, covenants, refinancing windows, and likely recovery value across scenarios.
- Prefer situations where downside is bounded by assets or survival odds while upside comes from normalization.
- Downgrade when bankruptcy or dilution risk is not compensated, when creditors control the outcome, or when policy support is already priced in.
- Separate "cheap because broken" from "cheap because the market is extrapolating temporary stress."

## George Soros Lens

Focus on reflexivity, macro feedback loops, and regime shifts.

- Ask how prices, fundamentals, policy, and investor behavior reinforce or destabilize each other.
- Identify whether a boom/bust process, currency move, credit cycle, commodity shock, or regulatory response can change the company's economics.
- Prefer asymmetric macro setups where the market is misreading a self-reinforcing trend or an approaching break in that trend.
- Downgrade when the thesis treats macro as a static backdrop, ignores positioning, or cannot define what would invalidate the regime call.
- This lens can support either a long or short if feedback loops create large non-linear outcomes.

## Howard Marks Lens

Focus on second-level thinking, cycles, and risk control.

- Ask what the consensus believes, what is already in the price, and where the market sits in the cycle.
- Prefer assets where pessimism, poor liquidity, or risk aversion create excess compensation for risk.
- Distinguish risk from volatility: permanent loss, weak covenants, leverage, and bad timing matter more than price movement alone.
- Choose offense when prospective returns are high and risk is well paid; choose defense when optimism and easy money dominate.
- Downgrade when the thesis is first-level, depends on benign cycles continuing, or lacks a margin for adverse conditions.

## Jim Simons Lens

Focus on statistical edge, base rates, robustness, and signal decay.

- Translate the thesis into observable signals and ask whether the edge would survive out-of-sample testing.
- Prefer evidence with repeatable base rates, large samples, low data-mining risk, and clear transaction-cost awareness.
- Treat stories as hypotheses; require measurable confirmation from price behavior, fundamentals, revisions, flows, or alternative data.
- Downgrade when the pattern is anecdotal, overfit, crowded, capacity-constrained, or likely arbitraged away.
- This lens should often return "insufficient data" rather than force a narrative judgment.

## Joel Greenblatt Lens

Focus on quality value and special situations.

- Look for high returns on capital available at attractive earnings yields, plus situations the market may temporarily misunderstand.
- Prefer spinoffs, restructurings, recapitalizations, forced selling, hidden assets, and simple businesses with durable economics.
- Ask whether the company ranks well on both business quality and valuation, not only one dimension.
- Downgrade when accounting earnings overstate cash earnings, returns on capital are mean-reverting, or the situation lacks a value-unlocking path.
- This lens can accept ugly or ignored companies when mechanics create a definable mispricing.

## John Templeton Lens

Focus on global contrarian value and maximum pessimism.

- Compare valuation, quality, and risk across countries and industries rather than staying in the investor's home market.
- Prefer companies or markets priced for despair where balance sheets and long-term earning power can survive.
- Ask whether pessimism is cyclical, political, currency-driven, or permanently justified by structural decline.
- Downgrade when cheapness is caused by unfixable governance, capital controls, permanent impairment, or unreliable shareholder rights.
- This lens is allowed to favor uncomfortable geographies or industries when pessimism is excessive and evidence is primary.

## Julian Robertson Lens

Focus on Tiger-style long/short quality, management, and relative winners.

- Pair business judgment with market discipline: own superior companies with strong management and short structurally weak competitors when evidence supports it.
- Prefer companies with clear competitive advantage, management credibility, accelerating fundamentals, and industry share shift.
- Ask which company in the ecosystem is the true winner and which is losing economics, customers, or strategic position.
- Downgrade when valuation ignores execution risk, when management quality is unproven, or when the position is crowded without improving fundamentals.
- This lens should surface relative-value comparisons, not only absolute buy/pass judgments.

## Li Lu Lens

Focus on quality value, long-term compounding, and China/Asia governance context.

- Prefer understandable businesses with durable moats, aligned owners, conservative balance sheets, and long reinvestment runways.
- Ask whether management treats minority shareholders fairly and whether reported economics convert into durable cash flow.
- For China and Asia-linked companies, examine policy direction, regulatory boundaries, related-party risk, capital allocation, and ownership structure.
- Downgrade when governance is opaque, leverage is hidden, policy risk can permanently impair economics, or the thesis rests on headline growth without cash conversion.
- This lens should distinguish macro discomfort from company-specific permanent loss risk.

## Michael Burry Lens

Focus on contrarian mispricing and forensic downside.

- Look for consensus complacency, misunderstood assets, hidden leverage, accounting stress, liquidity pressure, or crowded narratives.
- Prefer hard evidence that the market is ignoring: cash flow deterioration, balance sheet fragility, credit signals, or segment economics.
- For bullish ideas, require deep value or asymmetric downside protection.
- For bearish ideas, require a clear mechanism and timing risk awareness.
- Downgrade when the thesis is contrarian only for style, lacks a catalyst, or cannot survive a long wait.

## Mohnish Pabrai Lens

Focus on Dhandho-style low-risk, high-uncertainty bets.

- Ask whether downside is limited and upside is meaningfully asymmetric.
- Prefer simple businesses, copied proven playbooks, owner-operators, distressed but survivable situations, and clear margin of safety.
- Separate uncertainty from risk: uncertainty is acceptable; permanent capital loss is not.
- Downgrade when leverage can force failure, the business is too complex, or the price does not compensate for uncertainty.

## Nassim Taleb Lens

Focus on fragility and tail risk.

- Ask whether the company can survive extreme shocks without forced dilution, refinancing, or ruin.
- Prefer antifragile or convex exposures: limited downside, optional upside, cash-rich balance sheets, resilient demand.
- Penalize leverage, hidden liabilities, model risk, supply concentration, regulatory cliff risk, and dependence on stable conditions.
- Do not average away ruin risk with expected value language.
- A high-confidence idea can still be rejected if downside includes permanent impairment.

## Nick Sleep Lens

Focus on scale economies shared and patient owner-oriented compounding.

- Ask whether the company lowers cost or improves value for customers as it scales, strengthening the flywheel rather than merely extracting margin.
- Prefer simple businesses with long runways, customer obsession, reinvestment discipline, and management willing to defer profits for durable value creation.
- Look for evidence of widening customer value, lower prices, higher frequency, stronger retention, or ecosystem depth.
- Downgrade when growth comes from customer exploitation, financial engineering, subsidy dependence, or short-term margin maximization that weakens the moat.
- This lens can tolerate near-term accounting under-earning when customer value creation and unit economics are demonstrably improving.

## Peter Lynch Lens

Focus on understandable growth at a reasonable price.

- Explain the business in plain language and classify the story: slow grower, stalwart, fast grower, cyclical, turnaround, or asset play.
- Prefer visible growth, simple unit economics, strong same-store/customer/product evidence, and a valuation that does not outrun growth.
- Use PEG and cash generation as sanity checks, not as mechanical formulas.
- Downgrade when the story is exciting but earnings are shrinking, growth is already priced in, or the business is outside ordinary understanding.

## Phil Fisher Lens

Focus on exceptional long-term growth quality.

- Look for large addressable markets, product excellence, R&D strength, sales capability, management depth, culture, and customer trust.
- Prefer companies that can reinvest at high returns for many years.
- Require evidence beyond financial statements: customer traction, product roadmaps, competitive position, and management candor.
- Downgrade when growth depends on one product, one customer, one cycle, weak execution, or deteriorating innovation quality.

## Rakesh Jhunjhunwala Lens

Focus on emerging-market compounding, cycles, and management conviction.

- Look for long domestic-demand runways, rising formalization, market-share gain, and companies that can scale through cycles.
- Prefer strong promoters or management teams with execution history and aligned ownership.
- Accept cyclicality when the cycle, balance sheet, and valuation create asymmetric opportunity.
- Downgrade when governance is weak, leverage is high, liquidity is poor, or the thesis depends on macro optimism without company execution.

## Seth Klarman Lens

Focus on absolute-return value, distress, and margin of safety.

- Start with what can go wrong and demand a price that protects against adverse scenarios.
- Prefer cash, assets, liquidation value, distressed debt/equity, litigation or restructuring mispricings, and situations with limited downside.
- Treat holding cash as a valid decision when opportunity is poor; avoid being forced into relative-value optimism.
- Downgrade when downside is vague, leverage can force permanent loss, liquidity is poor without compensation, or valuation relies on heroic terminal assumptions.
- This lens should be skeptical of "quality at any price" and of crowded narratives with no hard asset or cash-flow support.

## Stanley Druckenmiller Lens

Focus on macro regime, liquidity, timing, and asymmetry.

- Ask whether fundamentals, liquidity, positioning, and price trend are aligned.
- Prefer setups with asymmetric upside/downside and a clear reason the market may reprice soon.
- Watch rates, currency, credit, fiscal policy, commodity cycles, capital flows, and sector leadership.
- Downgrade when the company is good but the macro tape is hostile, the position is crowded, or downside is not tightly controlled.

## Terry Smith Lens

Focus on high-quality compounding and disciplined inactivity.

- Prefer companies with consistently high returns on capital, strong gross margins, pricing power, recurring or repeat demand, and clean cash conversion.
- Ask whether the business can reinvest or return capital without destroying value and whether accounting profit is backed by free cash flow.
- Avoid banks, commodity producers, highly levered cyclicals, and businesses that require constant acquisition or financial engineering to grow.
- Downgrade when ROIC is deteriorating, growth consumes too much capital, margins are fragile, or valuation leaves no room for ordinary disappointment.
- This lens should favor "do nothing" ownership only when quality, durability, and valuation discipline all remain intact.

## Serenity Lens

Focus on value-chain bottlenecks and evidence.

- Start from the market story, then identify the real system change.
- Map the value chain and rank scarce layers before ranking companies.
- Prefer low supplier count, long qualification, hard expansion, customer certification, material purity, specialized equipment, or long lead times.
- Require public evidence: filings, announcements, transcripts, patents, standards, regulatory/project documents, customer cross-checks, or credible trade sources.
- Downgrade companies that mainly borrow a hot theme without controlling or sitting close to the constrained layer.

## Lens Selection

- Use all lenses for full company analysis.
- For quick screens, use Buffett, Munger, Graham, Damodaran, Taleb, and one context-specific lens.
- For growth companies, include Cathie Wood, Lynch, Fisher, Damodaran, and Taleb.
- For activist or governance situations, include Ackman, Buffett, Munger, and Damodaran.
- For contrarian or short ideas, include Burry, Graham, Taleb, Damodaran, and technicals.
- For quantitative or factor-style questions, include Jim Simons and Greenblatt, then verify data sufficiency before ranking.
- For special situations, spinoffs, restructurings, or distressed ideas, include Greenblatt, Klarman, Tepper, Burry, and Marks.
- For macro-sensitive names, include Druckenmiller, Soros, Taleb, Marks, and Rakesh Jhunjhunwala where emerging markets matter.
- For global or emerging-market value, include Templeton, Li Lu, Rakesh Jhunjhunwala, Graham, and Taleb.
- For high-quality compounders, include Buffett, Munger, Fisher, Nick Sleep, Terry Smith, and Li Lu.
- For portfolio construction or allocation questions, include Swensen, Marks, Taleb, Druckenmiller, and Damodaran.
- For long/short or relative-value comparisons, include Julian Robertson, Greenblatt, Burry, Druckenmiller, and technicals.
- For theme scans, start with Serenity, then apply company-level lenses to finalists.
- For hold/sell reviews, include Buffett sell criteria, Munger inversion, Damodaran valuation gap, Druckenmiller timing, and Taleb tail risk.

## Specialist Lenses

Use these as functional specialists rather than named investor personas:

- **Fundamentals**: profitability, growth, financial health, accounting quality, balance sheet, and capital efficiency.
- **Valuation**: DCF, owner earnings, multiples, residual income, scenario range, and market-implied expectations.
- **Technicals**: trend, momentum, mean reversion, volatility, relative strength, and risk timing.
- **Growth**: revenue durability, unit economics, TAM, reinvestment runway, and margin expansion.
- **News sentiment**: current company-specific news tone, source quality, and event impact.
- **Market sentiment**: insider activity, positioning, crowding, liquidity, and narrative heat.
