# Benjamin Graham Framework

## Lens Focus

asset protection, earnings power, conservative valuation, and margin of safety.

## Core Question

What conservative value can be proven from assets and normalized earnings, and how much margin of safety exists at the price?

## Decision Sequence

1. Start with the balance sheet, liquidity, liabilities, and earnings history.
2. Compute conservative asset value and normalized earnings power.
3. Stress liquidity and earnings under adverse conditions.
4. Demand a clear discount to conservative value.
5. Reject if debt or instability can destroy the margin of safety.
6. Prefer a portfolio of protected bargains over a single heroic forecast.

## Required Evidence

- Current assets, liabilities, debt, and tangible book value
- Five-to-ten-year earnings and dividend record
- Current ratio, interest coverage, and debt maturities
- Price to book, earnings, net current assets, and liquidation estimates
- Evidence of asset impairment or hidden liabilities

## Positive Pattern

- Low price to conservative assets or earnings
- Strong liquidity
- Modest leverage
- Stable multi-year earnings
- Dividend or tangible value support
- Clear discount to value

## Negative Pattern

- Weak current ratio
- Excessive debt
- Volatile earnings
- Premium valuation
- Unproven future growth
- Accounting opacity

## Time Horizon

medium-term value realization with downside protection; patience is acceptable only when the balance sheet survives.

## Signal Calibration

- Use `bullish` only when the positive pattern is backed by primary or cross-checked evidence and valuation does not negate it.
- Use `watch` when the lens likes the setup but needs price, catalyst, primary evidence, or a cleaner downside estimate.
- Use `pass`, `bearish`, or `sell-risk` when the negative pattern is central to the thesis.
- Use `too-hard` when the evidence does not let this lens form an edge.
