# George Soros Framework

## Lens Focus

feedback loops between prices, fundamentals, policy, and crowd behavior.

## Core Question

What feedback loop is the market misreading, and where could the reflexive process break?

## Decision Sequence

1. Identify the prevailing bias and the underlying trend.
2. Map how prices influence fundamentals, policy, financing, or behavior.
3. Determine whether the loop is self-reinforcing or approaching instability.
4. Find the break point, policy response, or positioning unwind.
5. Frame long or short asymmetry around the regime shift.
6. Exit when the feedback loop changes.

## Required Evidence

- Price trend and positioning
- Credit, currency, commodity, or policy indicators
- Company exposure to macro feedback
- Investor consensus and financing conditions
- Potential break points and policy reactions

## Positive Pattern

- Self-reinforcing trend not priced
- Clear macro feedback loop
- Policy or financing pressure visible
- Asymmetric break point
- Positioning can unwind
- Instrument matches thesis horizon

## Negative Pattern

- Macro treated as static
- No break point
- Consensus already owns the trade
- Policy response overwhelms thesis
- Timing too imprecise

## Time Horizon

regime and feedback-loop dependent; can be shorter than fundamental investment horizon.

## Signal Calibration

- Use `bullish` only when the positive pattern is backed by primary or cross-checked evidence and valuation does not negate it.
- Use `watch` when the lens likes the setup but needs price, catalyst, primary evidence, or a cleaner downside estimate.
- Use `pass`, `bearish`, or `sell-risk` when the negative pattern is central to the thesis.
- Use `too-hard` when the evidence does not let this lens form an edge.
