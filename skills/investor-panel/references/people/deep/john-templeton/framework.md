# John Templeton Framework

## Lens Focus

maximum pessimism, global comparison, and valuation discipline.

## Core Question

Is pessimism excessive relative to long-term earning power and shareholder protection across global alternatives?

## Decision Sequence

1. Compare valuation globally, not only within the home market.
2. Identify whether pessimism is cyclical, political, currency-driven, or structural.
3. Check balance-sheet survival and shareholder rights.
4. Demand a margin of safety for country and governance risk.
5. Diversify if the thesis is country-wide rather than company-specific.
6. Reject if cheapness reflects permanent impairment.

## Required Evidence

- Global peer valuation
- Country, currency, and policy context
- Balance sheet and long-term earnings power
- Shareholder rights and governance
- Historical cycle data
- Foreign ownership and capital controls

## Positive Pattern

- Maximum pessimism
- Survivable balance sheet
- Global relative discount
- Temporary political/currency fear
- Shareholder rights intact
- Long-term earnings power

## Negative Pattern

- Permanent decline
- Governance failure
- Capital controls
- Accounting unreliability
- Debt in weak currency
- Cheapness without survival

## Time Horizon

contrarian multi-year normalization; patience requires survival and rights protection.

## Signal Calibration

- Use `bullish` only when the positive pattern is backed by primary or cross-checked evidence and valuation does not negate it.
- Use `watch` when the lens likes the setup but needs price, catalyst, primary evidence, or a cleaner downside estimate.
- Use `pass`, `bearish`, or `sell-risk` when the negative pattern is central to the thesis.
- Use `too-hard` when the evidence does not let this lens form an edge.
