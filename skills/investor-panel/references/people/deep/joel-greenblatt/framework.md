# Joel Greenblatt Framework

## Lens Focus

high return on capital, earnings yield, and overlooked special situations.

## Core Question

Is this a good business or special situation available at an attractive yield because the market is temporarily ignoring it?

## Decision Sequence

1. Calculate whether the business earns high returns on capital.
2. Check whether earnings yield is attractive on normalized cash earnings.
3. Identify special-situation mechanics if present.
4. Ask who is forced to sell or ignore the security.
5. Verify that accounting earnings translate to cash.
6. Reject cheapness without quality or a value-unlocking path.

## Required Evidence

- EBIT and tangible capital
- Enterprise value and earnings yield
- Cash conversion and maintenance capex
- Spinoff/restructuring documents
- Ownership and forced-selling dynamics
- Hidden assets or segment values

## Positive Pattern

- High return on capital
- Attractive earnings yield
- Forced selling or neglect
- Clear event mechanics
- Cash earnings
- Simple business

## Negative Pattern

- Low-quality cheapness
- No cash conversion
- Mean-reverting returns
- No catalyst or mechanics
- Complex accounting
- Overcrowded special situation

## Time Horizon

one-to-three-year value realization for special situations; longer for quality-value compounding.

## Signal Calibration

- Use `bullish` only when the positive pattern is backed by primary or cross-checked evidence and valuation does not negate it.
- Use `watch` when the lens likes the setup but needs price, catalyst, primary evidence, or a cleaner downside estimate.
- Use `pass`, `bearish`, or `sell-risk` when the negative pattern is central to the thesis.
- Use `too-hard` when the evidence does not let this lens form an edge.
