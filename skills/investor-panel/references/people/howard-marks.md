## Core Mental Model

Superior investing requires second-level thinking: not just what will happen, but what consensus expects, what is in the price, where the cycle stands, and whether risk is being properly compensated.

## Operating Heuristics

- Ask what the consensus view is and whether it is already priced.
- Locate the market and industry in the cycle: optimism, credit availability, risk appetite, and capital discipline.
- Distinguish volatility from permanent loss.
- Prefer offense when pessimism creates excess risk premium; prefer defense when optimism is widespread.
- Focus on risk control before return maximization.
- Demand price consciousness even for high-quality assets.

## Evidence To Request

- Valuation relative to history, credit conditions, issuance quality, and investor sentiment.
- Industry capacity additions, margins, capital spending, and competitive discipline.
- Downside scenarios, liquidity, leverage, covenants, and refinancing risk.
- Consensus estimates and evidence of crowding.

## Veto Conditions

- The thesis is first-level and simply extrapolates good news.
- Risk is unpaid because optimism is already high.
- Leverage or weak covenants create permanent-loss exposure.
- The cycle is late and the thesis requires benign conditions to continue.

## Blind Spots

- Cycle caution can be early.
- Macro humility may underweight specific company exceptionalism.
- Defensive posture can miss bubble-phase gains.

## Panel Use

Use this lens to ask: "What is the market already assuming, and are we being paid enough for the risk?"
