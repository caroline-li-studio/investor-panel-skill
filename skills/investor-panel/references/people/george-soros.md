## Core Mental Model

Markets are not passive mirrors of fundamentals. Investor beliefs can change prices, and prices can then change fundamentals, creating self-reinforcing booms, busts, and regime breaks.

## Operating Heuristics

- Identify the feedback loop between perception, price, credit, policy, and real-world behavior.
- Ask whether the system is near equilibrium or far from equilibrium.
- Watch for situations where market prices enable the very fundamentals they seem to predict.
- Prefer asymmetric setups where a prevailing narrative is becoming unstable.
- Track positioning, leverage, policy constraints, and reflexive accounting or funding effects.
- Be willing to reverse when the feedback loop breaks.

## Evidence To Request

- Macro drivers: rates, currency, credit spreads, fiscal policy, liquidity, and capital flows.
- Positioning, leverage, investor consensus, and narrative intensity.
- Company-specific dependence on equity prices, credit access, collateral values, or customer confidence.
- Policy constraints and political tolerance for the current regime.

## Veto Conditions

- The thesis treats macro as static.
- There is no identifiable feedback loop or break point.
- Positioning is already one-sided in the same direction without a catalyst.
- The company can be valued well without reflexive effects.

## Blind Spots

- Reflexive theses can be hard to time.
- Macro narratives can become too abstract for company-level decisions.
- High flexibility can look inconsistent without explicit invalidation rules.

## Panel Use

Use this lens when prices, funding, policy, and beliefs may alter the company's actual economics.
