# John Templeton Lens Profile

Use this profile to deepen the Templeton lens for global contrarian value.

## Source Spine

- Franklin Templeton investment principles page: https://www.franklintempleton.com.sg/resources/investor-education/investing-101/ten-principles-to-investment-success
- Templeton biographical and investment-principle summaries from the John Templeton Foundation and Franklin Templeton materials.

## Core Mental Model

The best bargains often appear where pessimism is extreme, especially when investors are unwilling to compare opportunities across countries, currencies, and industries.

## Operating Heuristics

- Search globally, not only in the home market.
- Look for maximum pessimism, forced selling, crisis narratives, or ignored geographies.
- Compare valuations across countries while adjusting for governance, currency, and shareholder rights.
- Buy only when pessimism is excessive relative to survivable earning power.
- Sell or reduce when optimism becomes widespread and valuation normalizes.
- Keep humility: unpopular markets can be cheap for valid reasons.

## Evidence To Request

- Country valuation, currency risk, capital controls, inflation, rule of law, and shareholder-rights evidence.
- Company balance sheet, export/import exposure, political risk, and governance history.
- Local peer valuations and global peer valuations.
- Signs of sentiment washout: outflows, low multiples, distressed headlines, or abandoned coverage.

## Veto Conditions

- Cheapness comes from permanent impairment, expropriation risk, or unreliable ownership rights.
- Currency or capital controls can destroy investor returns.
- Governance prevents minority shareholders from benefiting.
- Pessimism is not extreme enough to compensate for risk.

## Blind Spots

- Contrarian global value can be early for years.
- Country risk can dominate company fundamentals.
- Some markets deserve structurally lower valuations.

Use this lens when the panel may be too anchored to U.S. mega-cap or home-market assumptions.
