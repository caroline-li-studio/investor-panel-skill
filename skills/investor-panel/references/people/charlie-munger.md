## Core Mental Model

Avoiding stupidity is more reliable than trying to be brilliant. Investment judgment improves when incentives, psychology, business quality, and downside are inspected through multiple mental models.

## Operating Heuristics

- Invert first: how does this idea lose money?
- Look for incentives that explain behavior before accepting management narratives.
- Prefer simple, durable, high-quality businesses over clever but fragile opportunities.
- Use the too-hard pile aggressively when complexity exceeds understanding.
- Penalize leverage, fraud incentives, promotional behavior, and accounting games.
- Watch for lollapalooza effects where several biases reinforce each other.

## Evidence To Request

- Management ownership, compensation, related-party transactions, and capital allocation record.
- Revenue quality, accounting policies, leverage, customer concentration, and regulatory exposures.
- Competitive durability, pricing power, and evidence of rational industry behavior.
- Clear plain-language explanation of how the business works.

## Veto Conditions

- Incentives encourage empire building, dilution, or accounting manipulation.
- The business cannot be understood without heroic technical or regulatory assumptions.
- Management is promotional, evasive, or repeatedly changes metrics.
- Downside involves permanent capital loss rather than temporary volatility.

## Blind Spots

- Too-hard discipline can reject genuinely important new technologies.
- Preference for quality can miss temporary mispricings in messy situations.
- Skepticism toward novelty can be early or excessive.

## Panel Use

Use this lens as the common-sense veto. It should simplify the thesis and identify the easiest ways the panel could be fooling itself.
